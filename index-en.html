Approach

How I work

Every project starts with diagnosis: understanding the context, mapping who's going to learn, and finding where knowledge gets stuck — in the content, the language, the sequence, or the way it's used.

With that in hand, I design and produce the solution: learning objectives, script, storyboard, and the final material, whether that's a course, a learning path, or an e-learning module. Then I refine it against real use.

Fifteen years of designing learning taught me that the result lives where pedagogical rigor meets product thinking. The techniques that get someone to learn — lowering cognitive load, giving feedback at the right moment, building a progression that keeps motivation alive — are the same ones that keep a user engaged in a digital product.

My Own EdTech Platform

Questopia

An English-learning platform (EFL) built on spaced repetition, gamification, and AI

Status: MVP in iteration Role: Founder & Learning Designer Nature: a research-grounded project in Design

The educational challenge

Teaching English as a foreign language in Brazil runs into a retention problem. A student studies vocabulary and grammar, gets tested once, and moves on — with no structured review. Cognitive science points to spaced repetition as one of the most effective methods for long-term retention, but the tools that exist, like Anki, demand a discipline most learners can't keep up on their own.

Two sides of the problem

EFL learner

Retains little vocabulary and few structures; has several activities competing for study time; drops anything that needs setup or solo discipline.

Teacher / language school

Spends time on repetitive review; has no data on the class's mistakes; needs something that complements the lesson rather than replacing it.

What shaped the design

Talking to learners surfaced a pattern: anything that competes with free time gets dropped. Review has to fit into the gaps in the day, with the lowest possible barrier to entry.

Talking to teachers surfaced something else: the tool only sticks if it creates value for whoever teaches — error data, saved time. The teacher is the one who recommends it to students.

Design decisions

Short review sessions

Five to ten minutes, grounded in microlearning and spaced repetition, so they fit the routine instead of competing with it.

Support the teacher, don't replace them

They're the adoption channel; if they feel their role is threatened, they won't recommend it.

Focus on EFL for Portuguese speakers

An audience with real urgency — work, travel, certification — and none of the compliance or parental-consent barriers of a younger audience.

Gamification and AI

Progression and instant feedback for engagement; AI to tune level and content to each learner's performance.

Iterations

Iteration 1 — Web MVP (ages 10–14)

The first working prototype at questopia.com.br. Testing showed low adoption: without clear urgency, spaced review lost out to more appealing activities. On top of that came the parental-consent barrier for academic research.

Iteration 2 — Audience pivot (current phase)

Guided by adoption data, I shifted toward people learning English as a foreign language — Portuguese-speaking adults and teens, with real urgency and their own motivation to review. The core of the product, spaced repetition with low friction, stayed the same; the audience changed.

Experience in Corporate and Digital Learning

Large-Scale Distance Learning

Teacher Training — Ministry of Education of Chile

International project · In partnership with Kyklos · 2017

Challenge: Teachers in Chile had to adopt a new methodology, but traditional training didn't scale and retention was low.

Solution: I designed a program grounded in instructional design and active learning — structured sessions, support materials, and follow-up after the training. I adapted the methodologies to the local cultural context, coordinated multiple stakeholders, and tracked performance indicators.

Result: The first groups reached 250 to 300 teacher-facilitators, with a design built to replicate at scale. It confirmed something simple: training works when the design shortens the distance between learning and doing.

Social-Impact Educational Program

Pró-Criança — Educational program design

Social impact · around 100 children · 2019

Challenge: A program that already existed but ran on low engagement, with outcomes below what was expected. It lacked a journey to carry participants from first contact to practice.

Solution: I redesigned the journey around the moments that matter — a clear welcome at the start, gradual progression, and steady feedback. I applied learning psychology to lower cognitive load and hold retention, with a curriculum built around learner autonomy.

Result: Engagement and completion rose noticeably, reaching around 100 children. The principles of good educational design — remove friction, create value early, stay with the journey — hold just as well in a school, in a company, and online.
