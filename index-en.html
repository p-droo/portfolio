How I Work

I apply learning psychology and instructional design principles to solve adoption problems: onboarding, time-to-value, and long-term engagement.

In 15 years designing learning experiences, I learned that adoption is an education problem — users need to understand the value, know how to get there, and feel progress along the way. The same techniques that work for teaching (reducing cognitive load, creating feedback loops, designing progressive journeys) work for activating and retaining product users.

Featured Case

Questopia

Reducing adoption friction through channel strategy

Status: MVP in iteration — testing WhatsApp delivery channel Role: Founder & PM (solo)

The Problem

Three stakeholders, three pain points:

Student

Low content retention. Multiple activities competing for their time — any solution requiring significant effort will be abandoned.

Teacher

Hours spent weekly grading recurring assignments. Little time for personalized teaching. No data on where students struggle.

Parents

No visibility into whether their children are studying outside of school.

Discovery

Research with students

  • Mapped daily routines and jobs to be done
  • Key insight: solutions that compete with free time get abandoned. The product must fit into gaps between existing activities.

Research with teachers

  • Mapped workflow and operational pain points
  • Key insight: the product must assist the teacher, not replace them. Time saved on grading is a tangible value proposition.
  • Hypothesis: significant reduction in weekly grading time (validation in progress via quantitative survey with 8-10 teachers).

Design Decisions

7-15 minute sessions

Based on microlearning principles and student journey mapping. Long enough to generate learning, short enough not to compete with other activities.

Assist, not replace the teacher

A decision driven by research. The teacher is the adoption channel — if they perceive the product as a threat, they won't adopt it.

Three distinct value propositions

Student: frictionless retention. Teacher: time savings + error data. Parents: oversight and visibility.

Gamification for engagement

Progression mechanics and immediate feedback to keep students engaged across short spaced repetition sessions.

Iteration 1 — Web MVP

Built a functional prototype using Lovable (questopia.com.br). Features: sign-up, review sessions, internal feedback system.

Results

  • First users tested the platform
  • Engagement below expectations
  • Internal feedback system received no messages
  • School seasonality (end/start of year) impacted testing

Diagnosis

The main adoption barrier wasn't content — it was the channel. Accessing a website, creating an account, and logging in is too much friction for K-8 students.

Iteration 2 — WhatsApp Pivot Current phase

Hypothesis

WhatsApp removes the main adoption barriers (login, download, sign-up). Students are already on the channel.

Approach

Manual testing before automation. Validate demand first, build backend later.

Test plan

  • Recruit 10-15 students through teachers
  • Send 1 review question/day via WhatsApp
  • Track: who responded, when, correct/incorrect
Success metrics:
  • Response rate >50% in the first 7 days
  • D7 retention >30%

Regulatory context

Brazil's CADE issued a preventive measure (Jan/2026) suspending Meta's restrictions against third-party AIs on WhatsApp. A specialized educational bot carries low regulatory risk — but the manual-first approach eliminates this dependency entirely.

Next steps

  • If validated: automate with self-hosted n8n
  • Define and track: response rate, D1/D7 retention, errors by topic

Enablement at Scale

Chile Project · Teacher Training

Problem: Teachers in Chile needed to adopt a new teaching methodology, but traditional training (in-person, one-off) didn't scale and generated low content retention.

Approach: Designed a training program applying instructional design and active learning principles — structured sessions, support materials, and post-training follow-up to ensure practical application.

Result: Teachers trained at scale with effective adoption of the new methodology in the classroom. The program demonstrated that enablement works when experience design reduces the friction between learning and applying.

Customer Education

Pro Criança · Educational Program Design

Problem: The existing educational program had low engagement and wasn't generating expected learning outcomes. It lacked a structured journey guiding participants from first contact to practical application.

Approach: Redesigned the learning journey focused on key value moments — clear onboarding, gradual progression, and continuous feedback. Applied learning psychology to reduce cognitive load and increase retention.

Result: Redesigned program with significant increase in engagement and completion. The experience reinforced that the same customer education principles — reducing friction, creating early value moments, supporting the journey — apply to both formal education and product adoption.
