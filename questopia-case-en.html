Context

Brazil's EdTech market tends to focus on two extremes: early literacy (young children) and college entrance exam prep (high school). Middle school — students aged 10 to 14 — sits in the "no man's land" and suffers from a lack of product innovation.

At this age, content retention is a real problem. Students learn a topic, are tested once, and move on — with no structured review. Cognitive science research shows that spaced repetition is one of the most effective methods for long-term retention, but existing products (like Anki) demand discipline and autonomy that 10-14 year olds simply don't have.

On the teacher side, the situation is equally problematic: hours spent weekly grading recurring assignments, little time for personalized teaching, and almost no data on student error patterns.

My Thesis

People learn and retain better when the journey is designed with intentionality. Spaced repetition is one of the most proven methods in cognitive science — but existing products demand discipline that 10-14 year olds don't have.

My bet: it's possible to deliver effective spaced repetition if the product is designed to fit into the student's routine (not the other way around), if it's adopted by the teacher (not imposed), and if it provides visibility for parents.

Discovery

Research with Students

I conducted qualitative research with middle school students to understand how they organize their time, which activities compete for their attention, and how willing they'd be to use a review tool.

Key findings:

  • Multiple activities compete for the student's time: school, sports, screens, rest
  • Any solution requiring more than ~15 minutes per session would be abandoned
  • Strong resistance to anything that feels like "more homework"
  • The product needs to fit into gaps between existing activities, not create a new time slot

Research with Teachers

I researched teachers to map their workflow, understand operational pain points, and identify where a product could deliver real value.

Key findings:

  • Significant time spent grading recurring assignments
  • No data on student error patterns — grading happens, but the data is lost
  • Fear of being replaced by technology — any product that appears to threaten the teacher's role will be rejected

Hypothesis being validated: significant reduction in weekly grading time. Validation in progress via quantitative survey with 8-10 teachers.

Cross-cutting Insight

The product needs to be designed for the student to use, for the teacher to adopt, and for parents to monitor. Three interfaces, three value propositions, one product.

Product Decisions

7-15 Minute Sessions

Rationale: microlearning principles + student journey mapping. Research showed that sessions longer than 15 minutes are abandoned.

Trade-off: longer sessions could generate more learning per session, but the dropout rate would be far too high. I chose frequency over depth — spaced repetition works better with short, consistent repetition.

Assist, Not Replace the Teacher

Rationale: teacher research revealed that fear of replacement is a real adoption barrier.

Trade-off: limits product autonomy (can't function 100% without the teacher), but ensures adoption through the most important channel. The teacher is the one who recommends the product to students — if they don't adopt, the product never reaches the end user.

Three Distinct Value Propositions

Student Frictionless content retention — short sessions that fit into their routine.
Teacher Time saved on grading + data on where students make errors.
Parents Oversight and visibility into their children's studying outside of school.

Gamification for Engagement

Progression mechanics and immediate feedback designed to keep students engaged in short sessions. The goal isn't to create addiction — it's to lower the barrier to entry and maintain consistency over time.

Iteration 1: Web MVP

Tool: Lovable (no-code builder)
What was built: Web platform with sign-up, spaced review sessions, internal feedback system, and basic progress dashboard.
Available at: questopia.com.br

Results

  • First users tested the platform
  • Engagement below expectations
  • Internal feedback system received no messages
  • School seasonality (end/start of school year) impacted the testing window

Diagnosis

The main adoption barrier wasn't content — it was the channel. Accessing a website, creating an account, logging in, and navigating to a review session is too much friction for K-8 students. The product might be good, but if the delivery channel creates friction, the user never gets to experience it.

Iteration 2: WhatsApp Pivot

Hypothesis

WhatsApp removes the main adoption barriers: no download needed, no login needed, no sign-up needed. The student is already on the channel — the review question comes to them, not the other way around.

Approach

Manual testing before automation. Sending questions manually via WhatsApp to validate demand before investing time building a bot. If the hypothesis holds, I'll automate with self-hosted n8n.

Test Design

  • Recruit 10-15 students through partner teachers
  • Send 1 review question per day via WhatsApp
  • Track: who responded, when they responded, correct or incorrect
  • Planned duration: 2-3 weeks

Defined Metrics

  • Response rate: >50% in the first 7 days
  • D7 retention: >30% of students still responding after 7 days
  • Errors by topic: mapping subjects with the highest error rate

Regulatory Context

Brazil's CADE issued a preventive measure (January 2026) suspending Meta's restrictions against third-party AIs on WhatsApp. A specialized educational bot carries low regulatory risk — but the manual-first approach completely eliminates this dependency.

Status: preparing to execute the test.

Learnings

1. Channel matters more than content during adoption

The web MVP had adequate content, but the delivery channel created too much friction. Before iterating on the product, I needed to iterate on the channel.

2. Testing with few users isn't validation

Testing with 4 users and no feedback received doesn't generate actionable data. For the next iteration, I defined clear success metrics before starting the test.

3. The teacher is the adoption channel, not the student

To reach K-8 students, I need to convince the teacher first. The product needs to deliver value to them before it reaches the student.

4. Manual testing before automation

Building a bot before knowing if demand exists is waste. Sending messages manually costs time, but generates real behavioral data.

Next Steps

  • Execute WhatsApp test with 10-15 students
  • Analyze response rate, D1/D7 retention, and error patterns
  • If validated: automate with self-hosted n8n
  • Conduct quantitative survey with 8-10 teachers to validate time savings hypothesis
  • Iterate on question format (text, image, audio) based on collected data
