Context

Teaching English as a foreign language in Brazil runs into a retention problem. A student studies vocabulary and grammar, gets tested once, and moves on — with no structured review. Cognitive science points to spaced repetition as one of the most effective methods for long-term retention, but the tools that exist, like Anki, demand a discipline most learners can't keep up on their own.

On the teaching side it's just as hard: time spent on repetitive review, little visibility into the class's mistakes, and almost no data to personalize a learner's next step.

My Thesis

People learn and retain better when the journey is designed with intent. Spaced repetition is one of the most proven methods in cognitive science — but the products that apply it demand a discipline most learners can't keep up on their own.

My bet: you can deliver effective spaced review if the product is designed to fit into the gaps in a learner's day (not the other way around) and if it creates value for the teacher, who is the one who recommends the tool to students.

Discovery

Research with Learners

I ran qualitative research with English learners to understand how they organize study time, what competes for their attention, and how willing they are to use a review tool.

Key findings:

  • Anything that competes with free time gets dropped — review has to fit into the gaps in the day
  • Long sessions get abandoned; the barrier to entry has to be as low as possible
  • Intrinsic motivation (work, travel, certification) sustains the review habit far more than external obligation

Research with Teachers

I researched English teachers to map their workflow, understand their operational pain points, and find where a product could create real value.

Key findings:

  • Significant time spent on repetitive review
  • No data on the class's error patterns — grading happens, but the data is lost
  • Fear of being replaced by technology — any product that seems to threaten the teacher's role gets rejected

Hypothesis under validation: the tool only enters the routine if it creates value for whoever teaches (error data, saved time), because the teacher is the one who recommends it to students.

Cross-cutting Insight

The product has to be designed for the student to use and for the teacher to adopt. Two interfaces, two value propositions, one product — and it's the teacher who opens the door to the student.

Design Decisions

Short review sessions

Rationale: microlearning and spaced-repetition principles. Five-to-ten-minute sessions that fit the routine instead of competing with it.

Trade-off: longer sessions could produce more learning per sitting, but the drop-off rate would be high. I chose frequency over depth — spaced repetition works best with short, constant repetition.

Support the teacher, don't replace them

Rationale: research showed that fear of replacement is a real barrier to adoption.

Trade-off: it limits the product's autonomy, but it secures adoption through the most important channel. The teacher is the one who recommends the tool — if they don't adopt it, the product never reaches the student.

Focus on EFL for Portuguese speakers

Rationale: an audience with real urgency — work, travel, certification — and none of the compliance or parental-consent barriers of a younger audience.

Gamification and AI

Progression and instant feedback for engagement; AI to tune level and content to each learner's performance. The goal isn't to "hook" anyone — it's to lower the barrier to entry and keep consistency over time.

Student Frictionless retention of vocabulary and structures — short sessions that fit the routine.
Teacher Time saved on review plus data on where the class gets it wrong.

Iteration 1: Web MVP (ages 10–14)

Tool: Lovable (no-code builder)
What was built: a web platform with sign-up, spaced-repetition sessions, an internal feedback system, and a basic progress dashboard.
Live at: questopia.com.br

Results

  • First users tried the platform
  • Low adoption: without clear urgency, spaced review lost out to more appealing activities
  • Parental-consent barrier for academic research with minors

Diagnosis

The problem wasn't the method — it was the audience. Teens aged 10–14 don't have enough urgency to sustain a review habit, and research with minors runs into compliance and parental consent. The core of the product worked; what was missing was an audience with its own motivation to review.

Iteration 2: Audience Pivot (current phase)

Decision

Guided by adoption data, I shifted the focus toward people learning English as a foreign language — Portuguese-speaking adults and teens. The core of the product (spaced repetition with low friction) stayed the same; the audience changed.

Why the new audience sustains the habit

  • Real urgency: work, certifications, and travel give a concrete reason to review
  • Intrinsic motivation: the learner chooses to study on their own, without relying on school obligation
  • No parental-consent barriers — validating with users becomes simpler and faster

What I'm validating now

  • Habit retention: learners returning to review sessions week over week
  • Value for the teacher: time saved and error data that justify the recommendation
  • Errors by topic: mapping the highest-error topics to personalize review

Status: MVP in iteration with the new audience.

Lessons

1. The audience's urgency matters more than the method

The method (spaced repetition) was right; the initial audience lacked the urgency to sustain the habit. Before iterating on the product, I had to iterate on the audience.

2. Validating with few users isn't validating

Tests with a handful of users and no feedback received don't produce actionable data. For the current phase, I defined clear success metrics before starting.

3. The teacher is the adoption channel

To reach the student, I first have to create value for whoever teaches. The product has to save time and return error data before it gets recommended.

4. The right audience unlocks the research

Moving to a motivated adult audience removed the compliance and consent barriers and accelerated the validation cycle with real users.

Next Steps

  • Run the habit-retention validation with the new EFL audience
  • Analyze retention week over week and error patterns by topic
  • Research English teachers to validate the time-saving hypothesis
  • Use AI to tune level and content to each learner's performance
  • Iterate on question format (text, image, audio) based on the data collected
